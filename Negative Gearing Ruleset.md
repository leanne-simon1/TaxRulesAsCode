1. **Negative gearing ruleset (deductibility of rental property losses, and the enacted 2027 quarantine reform)**

> **EM-verified 2026-07-08; tax-specialist review still recommended.** Current law
> (s 8-1, s 25-10, s 25-25, s 26-31, s 26-102, Division 35, Division 43, s 110-45)
> verified against the authoritative ITAA 1997 text held locally (Federal Register
> `C2004A05138`; see `research/negative_gearing/README.md`). The co-ownership splitting
> rule is verified against *FC of T v McDonald* (1987) 18 ATR 957 and ATO Taxation Ruling
> TR 93/32. The enacted negative-gearing quarantine (section 4 below) is part of the
> **same Act as the CGT reform** — the **Treasury Laws Amendment (Tax Reform No. 1) Act
> 2026** (`C2026A00049`), assented 26 June 2026 — but is a **separate schedule**
> (Schedule 2, "Limit negative gearing for residential property to new builds") verified
> here against the Bill's **Explanatory Memorandum**, Chapter 2, rather than the enacted
> Schedule 2 body text itself (not independently re-fetched this session — see the
> research doc for what that would take to close out). Section 4 rules are **enacted but
> not yet in effect**: they apply to net rental losses incurred in the 2027-28 income
> year and later.

   1. **Whether a rental loss can offset other income (current law)**

1. Rental expenses are deductible under the ordinary **general deduction** provision to
   the extent incurred in gaining or producing the rental income, excluding capital,
   private/domestic, or exempt-income-related outgoings.[^1]

2. Where an entity's rental deductions for a property exceed its rental income for the
   year, current law does **not** quarantine the excess: it is simply an ordinary
   deduction, reducing the entity's other assessable income (salary and wages, business
   or other investment income) for that year. There is no dedicated "negative gearing"
   provision — this is the *combined effect* of rule 1.1 and there being no quarantining
   rule that applies to it.[^2]

3. In particular, the **non-commercial-loss quarantining rule** (Division 35) does
   **not** apply to an ordinary rental property investment: Division 35 only defers
   losses from a **business activity**, and passively letting a residential property is
   not, of itself, "carrying on a business". An individual (or a company, trust or
   partnership — none of which Division 35 applies to at all) letting a residential
   property therefore keeps the unrestricted offset in rule 1.2. This is the mechanism
   commonly called **negative gearing**.[^3]

4. Where a property is owned by more than one entity, the net rental income or loss is
   split according to each owner's **legal ownership interest** — not by any private
   agreement between the owners, and not by who actually paid the expenses. Co-owners
   are treated as co-owners of an investment, not as partners in a business, for this
   purpose.[^4]

   2. **What is and isn't deductible for a rental property (current law)**

1. **Repairs** to the rental premises (or a depreciating asset held for the rental
   purpose) are immediately deductible; **improvements** (capital expenditure) are not —
   they must instead be depreciated (rule 2.4) or written off under Division 43 (rule
   2.5).[^5]

2. **Borrowing expenses** (e.g. loan establishment fees) are deductible spread evenly
   over the period of the loan, capped at 5 years; the full amount is immediately
   deductible if the total for the year is $100 or less.[^6]

3. **Travel** to inspect or maintain a residential rental property, or to collect rent,
   is **not deductible**, unless the taxpayer is a corporate tax entity, a
   superannuation fund that is not a self managed superannuation fund, a managed
   investment trust, a public unit trust, or a trust/partnership every member of which is
   one of those (from 1 July 2017).[^7]

4. **Decline in value of a second-hand depreciating asset** (plant and equipment already
   installed or used by someone else when the taxpayer first held it) in residential
   rental premises is **not deductible**, with the same kind-of-entity exceptions as rule
   2.3, unless the asset is supplied new as part of newly built residential premises with
   no prior occupant (from 1 July 2017).[^8]

5. **Capital works** (the building's construction cost) are deductible at a flat rate
   over time under Division 43: **2.5% per year for up to 40 years** for construction
   begun after 26 February 1992 (this ruleset models only this, the standard modern
   case; earlier-construction rates are a documented simplification).[^9]

6. **Holding costs for vacant land** (including loan interest) are **not deductible**
   unless the land is used in carrying on a business (including primary production), or
   — for residential premises being built — the premises are lawfully occupiable and
   leased or available for lease (from 1 July 2019). Land held before this measure's
   start, or with an existing rentable structure, is unaffected.[^10]

7. Any of the above amounts that have been (or can be) deducted are **excluded from the
   property's CGT cost base** on eventual disposal (see `Capital Gains Tax Ruleset.md`
   rule 2.2) — negative gearing deductions taken today reduce the cost base, and so can
   increase the taxable capital gain, later.[^11]

   3. **The enacted negative-gearing quarantine (established dwellings, from 1 July
      2027)**

1. From the **2027-28 income year**, net rental losses from a **residential dwelling**
   used or held as residential accommodation are **quarantined** if the entity's
   ownership interest in it was acquired **on or after 7:30pm AEST on 12 May 2026** (the
   2026-27 Budget announcement): the excess deductions are **not** deductible against the
   entity's other assessable income for that year. Instead they can only be applied
   against (a) net assessable income from the entity's **non-quarantined** residential
   dwellings, or (b) revenue or capital gains on residential dwellings; any amount left
   over is **carried forward** to the next income year.[^12]

2. **Grandfathering.** The quarantine does **not** apply to a residential dwelling whose
   ownership interest was **last acquired before 7:30pm AEST 12 May 2026** — for a
   dwelling bought under contract, "acquired" means the contract date, not settlement,
   matching the ordinary CGT timing rule. Vacant land acquired before that time remains
   exempt even if a dwelling is built on it afterwards (though the separate vacant-land
   holding-cost denial, rule 2.6, can still apply in the meantime).[^13]

3. **New-build exception.** The quarantine does not apply to a **new residential
   dwelling** in relation to the entity. The precise definition is set by **Ministerial
   legislative instrument** (not yet made); the EM's non-binding guidance is a dwelling
   built on vacant land, or a rebuild that genuinely adds to housing supply (e.g. one
   demolished dwelling replaced by two separately titled dwellings) — replacing one
   dwelling with one does not qualify.[^14]

4. **Minister-determined exceptions.** The quarantine also does not apply to a dwelling
   used for an activity, purpose, business or enterprise the Minister determines by
   legislative instrument (the EM gives social/affordable housing rental as an example),
   nor to expenditure incurred providing a **fringe benefit** (e.g. employer-provided
   housing).[^15]

5. **Entity exceptions.** **Widely held trusts** (e.g. most managed investment trusts)
   and **complying superannuation entities — including self managed superannuation
   funds** — are never subject to the quarantine, nor is any entity class the Minister
   determines. *(This entity-exception set is different from rule 2.3/2.4's: those
   exclude a superannuation fund that is **not** an SMSF, so an SMSF gets no relief
   there, while it is fully excepted here. A company is not excepted from the quarantine
   at all — it is simply not named as one of the excepted entity types.)*[^16]

6. **Definition of residential dwelling.** A dwelling (in the existing CGT main-residence
   sense) other than a caravan or other mobile home, a hotel/motel/hostel/boarding house,
   student accommodation, or a boat, plus any adjacent land or structure (garage, shed,
   etc.) used with it — with the Minister able to exclude further classes by legislative
   instrument.[^17]

7. **Ordering and carry-forward.** Where a quarantined dwelling's excess exists for the
   year, it is first reduced by any net income from the entity's non-quarantined
   dwellings; any remainder is then applied against residential capital gains via two new
   steps inserted into the CGT net-capital-gain method statement (after capital losses,
   before the discount) — see `Capital Gains Tax Ruleset.md` rule 1.6.7; whatever is
   still left over carries forward to the next income year. A quarantined amount that has
   reduced a capital gain is excluded from the dwelling's CGT cost base, so it cannot
   also inflate the cost base (the negative-gearing-specific version of rule 2.7).[^18]

8. **Bankruptcy.** A quarantined amount that accrued before an entity is declared
   bankrupt cannot be deducted, or applied against a capital gain, after the bankruptcy —
   it is effectively extinguished, following the same pattern as the existing
   non-commercial-business-loss and boating-loss quarantining rules.[^19]

   4. **Terms**

| Term | Definition | References |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Negative gearing | Colloquial term for the position where an entity's deductions relating to a rental property investment exceed the rental income it produces, with the excess offsetting the entity's other assessable income | EM Ch 2 |
| General deduction | A loss or outgoing deductible because it was incurred in gaining or producing assessable income (and is not capital, private, or related to exempt income) | s 8-1 ITAA 1997 |
| Non-commercial loss (Division 35) | A rule deferring losses from a **business activity**, not applicable to passive rental letting, which is why negative gearing is available under current law | Div 35 ITAA 1997 |
| Ownership interest | When an entity becomes the owner of land/a dwelling, or gains a right/licence to occupy it — for a contract purchase, the contract date | s 118-130, s 109-5 ITAA 1997 |
| Residential dwelling | A dwelling used or held as residential accommodation (excludes caravans, hotels/motels, student accommodation, boats; includes adjacent land/structures) | New s 26-160 ITAA 1997 (Sch 2, Tax Reform No. 1 Act 2026) |
| New residential dwelling | A residential dwelling meeting a Minister-determined test (not yet made) for genuinely adding to housing supply — excepted from the quarantine | New s 26-160(3)-(5) ITAA 1997 |
| Quarantined amount | The part of a residential dwelling's net rental loss for the year that cannot be deducted against other income because of the 2027 reform, carried forward or applied to residential capital gains instead | New s 26-155 ITAA 1997 |
| Capital works deduction | The flat-rate (2.5%/4% per year) write-off of a rental building's construction cost | Div 43 ITAA 1997 |

   5. **PoC constraints and caveats**

* Scope is a **single dwelling per entity** for the primary calculation, with one
  aggregate input standing in for the net income of the entity's other, non-quarantined
  dwellings — full multi-property aggregation (as in the EM's worked examples) is not
  modelled property-by-property.
* The Ministerial legislative instruments (new-build definition, further
  Minister-determined dwelling/business/entity exceptions) do not exist yet — modelled as
  direct boolean inputs, not as computed logic, matching how `cgt.py` treats its own
  not-yet-defined new-build election.
* The s 25-25 borrowing-expense spread, mixed private/rental apportionment (a dwelling
  partly a main residence), the pre-1992 Division 43 rates, and the full s 26-102
  vacant-land exception set are simplified or taken as direct inputs — see
  `research/negative_gearing/README.md` for the detail each simplifies away.
* Section 3 must be encoded as **dated law** (parameters/formulas gated on the
  ownership-interest acquisition date and the income year), not as a separate ruleset —
  consistent with this repo's CGT-reform pattern.
* Outputs are guidance for exploring Rules as Code, not legal or tax advice; any
  user-facing surface must carry the same reliance caveats the ATO's own tools use.
