Late objection — extension of time (s 14ZX) sense check

When someone objects to a tax decision after the deadline has passed, they can ask the Commissioner for extra time under section 14ZX. Deciding whether to grant that extra time is a judgement call — you weigh up the person's circumstances, and no computer (or AI) should make that call for you. This tool does not decide it either. What it does is take care of the firm legal checks that sit around that judgement, so you can focus on the judgement itself. It:

  • Works out whether the objection really was late — if it was in time, there is nothing to extend and you don't need to decide the request at all.
  • Calculates how late it was, from the dates you enter.
  • Records your findings on each of the things the law asks you to weigh, with a reference to where each one comes from.
  • Sense-checks the decision you're leaning towards against patterns in decided cases, and raises a flag if something looks unusual — so you can make sure your written reasons deal with it. The flag never changes your decision.
Who this is for. This is a view for the case officer or reviewer, not the taxpayer. It assumes a late objection with a request for extra time has landed on your desk and you are drafting the decision. The plain-language rules behind it are in the Extension of Time Ruleset.md (DRAFT, pending tax specialist review).

Try it with three worked examples

Each button fills in the form below with a ready-made scenario and runs the check, so you can see the kinds of things the tool picks up. They cover the three situations it is most useful for: catching a request that shouldn't be decided at all, questioning a refusal that looks harsh, and questioning a grant that looks generous.

1 · The objection wasn't actually late

An officer is drafting a refusal of a request for extra time — but the tool works out that the objection was lodged in time. There is nothing to extend, so this request shouldn't be decided at all, no matter what its merits are. This is the kind of firm legal check the tool is there to catch.

2 · Refusing when everything points the other way

Three weeks late, the explanation covers the whole period, the objection is arguable, there's no real prejudice to the Commissioner, and the taxpayer didn't just sit on their rights — yet the draft says refuse. The tool doesn't overrule you; it flags that this looks unusual and asks you to spell out, in your reasons, what outweighs a set of facts that decided cases treat as strongly in the taxpayer's favour.

3 · Granting the classic refusal pattern

Two years late after sitting on their rights, with a gap in the explanation and nothing the ATO did to cause the delay — yet the draft says agree. The tool flags that this matches a pattern courts have usually refused, and asks what sets this case apart. Switch the proposal to refuse and run it again: the flag disappears — the weighing is yours.

2

Your findings on the circumstances

Your judgement — the tool only records it

These are the things PS LA 2003/7 asks you to weigh up when deciding whether to grant extra time. Make each finding yourself, from the material in front of you — the tool simply records what you decide and notes where each factor comes from. It does not work these out for you.

Consider whether the reasons cover the entire time the taxpayer was late, not just part of it. A gap in the explanation counts against them.

You are not deciding whether the objection would succeed — only whether it has some genuine basis and isn't hopeless. This is a low bar; no full merits investigation.

Consider whether the delay itself has caused a real disadvantage — for example lost records or faded memories. Extra work or inconvenience is not prejudice.

Consider whether ATO action or inaction — for example a delayed or unclear response — played a part in the taxpayer being late.

Consider whether the taxpayer knew about the decision and let the time pass without acting, rather than actively pursuing the matter.

3 Sense check on your decision

The tool has no answer of its own for whether to grant or refuse — that decision is yours, and it stays out of it entirely. Deciding a s 14ZX request means weighing everything together; there is no score, no checklist total and no single factor that settles it. All the tool's flags do is point out when a proposal either isn't legally available or looks unusual next to decided cases. If a flag comes up on a decision you're confident in, the answer is to explain it more clearly in your reasons — not to change your decision.

Keeping the decision with the officer is deliberate (rule 1.4.1 of the ruleset): a rule that dictated the outcome would be unlawful, because it would remove the very judgement the law requires a person to exercise.
