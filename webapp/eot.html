Extension of time to object (s 14ZX) — decision sense check

The s 14ZX discretion — whether to accept a late objection — is a judgement-based decision: a human weighs the factors, and no rules engine (or AI) should make it. But the discretion sits inside a deterministic legal shell, and this page shows the rules engine holding exactly that line: the engine decides the gatekeepers (is the request even validly before the Commissioner?), calculates the delay, records the officer's factor findings with legislative citations, and sense-checks a proposed outcome against decided-case patterns — while the grant/refuse call itself has no variable in the engine at all. This is the grounding a Copilot-style assistant needs: it can call these rules instead of free-generating law.

Who this is for. Unlike the objection calculator (a taxpayer view), this is an officer / reviewer view: it assumes a late objection with an extension request is on the desk and a decision is being drafted. Ruleset: Extension of Time Ruleset.md (DRAFT, pending tax specialist review).

Three worked examples

Each button fills the form below with a scenario and asks the live engine. The three examples demonstrate the three tiers of value: a hard jurisdictional catch, an atypical-refusal prompt, and an atypical-grant prompt.

1 · The request that isn't one

An officer drafts a refusal of an "extension request" — but the engine computes the objection was lodged in time. There is nothing to extend: deciding this request at all would be an error, whatever the merits. This is the deterministic Level 1–2 shell a Copilot must never get wrong — and with the engine, it can't.

2 · Refusing an all-favourable profile

Three weeks late, the explanation covers the whole period, the case is arguable, no concrete prejudice, no resting on rights — and the draft says refuse. The engine doesn't overrule anyone; it raises a flag asking the decision maker to articulate what outweighs a profile that decided cases treat as strongly favourable.

3 · Granting the classic refusal pattern

Two years late after resting on rights, with an explanation gap and no contributing ATO conduct — and the draft says agree. The engine flags the recurring refusal pattern and asks what displaces it. Switch the proposal to refuse and re-check: the flag goes away — the weighing is the human's.

3 The proposed decision — sense check

There is no variable in the engine for whether the request should be granted or refused. The s 14ZX discretion is evaluative and balancing — no scoring, no matrices, no decision tree, no factor determinative. The engine's flags mark a proposal as jurisdictionally unavailable or atypical against decided-case patterns; they never decide. If a flag fires on a decision you stand behind, the answer is better articulation in the reasons — not a different decision.

This boundary is rule 1.4.1 of the ruleset, and it is what keeps the discretion lawful: a mechanical rule that dictated the outcome would fetter it.
