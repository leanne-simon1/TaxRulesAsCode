Tax rules engine showcase

Small tools built on governed rulesets. The tax rules live once, in the OpenFisca rules engine — encoded from plain-language rulesets with legislative references attached to every rule. Each tool below is just a thin view over the same engine: none of them contain any tax logic of their own.

PAYG instalment variation

Variation calculator

A guided calculator for varying PAYG instalments: answer the BAS questions, the engine returns the estimated tax (T8), varied amount payable (T9) and new varied rate (T3) — each with a "why" citation drawn from the rule's own metadata. Includes a what-if sensitivity table computed in a single batched engine call, and a Show the working panel that renders the engine's full calculation trace — every rule that fired, with its values and policy parameters.

Objection rights (Part IVC TAA 1953)

Whether a taxpayer can object to an ATO decision, and by when — encoded from Objection Rights Ruleset.md and driven by the same engine, same conventions, same "why" citations as the PAYGI tools above.

Objection calculator

Answer questions about the ATO decision you're dissatisfied with — the engine returns whether you have an objection right, your deadline, whether you're in time, and the recommended pathway, each with its legislative reference. A built-in eligibility help panel summarises which decisions you can and can't object to and the time limits — generated live by the rules engine, in the style of the ATO's Eligibility to lodge an objection page.

Open objection calculator

Extension of time (s 14ZX) sense check

Can a rules engine help with a discretionary decision without making it? An officer-view tool for late-objection extension requests: the engine decides the deterministic gatekeepers (is a valid request even before the Commissioner?), calculates the delay, records the PS LA 2003/7 factor findings with citations, and sense-checks a proposed outcome against decided-case patterns — while the grant/refuse call itself deliberately has no variable in the engine. Three worked examples show how this grounds a Copilot-style assistant. Encoded from Extension of Time Ruleset.md.

Capital gains tax (2027 reform)

Compare the CGT cost of a disposal under today's 50% discount and the enacted 1 July 2027 rules (indexation + a 30% minimum tax), forecast future buys and sells, and see the grandfathered outcome for a gain that straddles the reform — encoded from Capital Gains Tax Ruleset.md.

CGT calculator

Enter an acquisition and disposal (past or future) and the engine returns the CGT cost under current rules, the new rules, and your actual grandfathered outcome, each with its legislative reference. Accounts for the 12-month discount, indexation, the 30% minimum tax, entity type, and the transitional split at 1 July 2027.

Negative gearing (2027 quarantine)

Whether a rental loss still offsets your other income, or is quarantined under the enacted 2027 reform — grandfathering by acquisition date, new-build exemptions, and the s 26-155(6) carry-forward waterfall, encoded from Negative Gearing Ruleset.md.

Negative gearing focus page

Four views over one ruleset: a grandfathering timeline showing where your acquisition falls, a before/after calculator comparing the same property under current law and the quarantine, a multi-property waterfall dashboard for a whole portfolio, and a chat panel that collects your facts and lets the engine decide — citing the legislation behind every answer.

Working Australians Tax Offset (from 2027-28)

The $250 WATO — whether you're entitled, how much you get, and why: an individual Australian resident whose net labour income exceeds $18,200, phasing in at 14c/$ to the full $250 — encoded from Working Australians Tax Offset Ruleset.md (new Subdivision 61-E ITAA 1997).

Worker offset (WATO) tools

Three ways to use one ruleset: an eligibility questionnaire that asks a question at a time and lets the engine decide against the four entitlement gates, an offset calculator whose phase-in curve is swept point-by-point through the engine in one batched call, and a show-the-working trace of every rule and dated parameter behind the answer.

Why this matters

  • One source of truth — calculator, explanation view, documentation and (later) chatbot all call the same governed rules via the same API.
  • Traceable to law — every rule carries a reference to ato.gov.au or legislation; answers arrive with their citations.
  • Maintainable by specialists — policy values (like the corporate tax rate) are dated YAML parameters, and the rules explorer gives non-coders a faithful view of what is encoded.
  • Testable — the ruleset ships with executable YAML test scenarios; CI blocks any change that breaks them.
