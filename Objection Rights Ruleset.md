1. **Objection rights ruleset (ATO decisions — review rights under Part IVC TAA 1953)**

> **DRAFT — pending tax specialist review.** Drafted 2026-07-06 from the sources in

> `legislation.gov.au` provision scrape (see `research/README.md` "Findings from

> Rules marked *(VERIFY)* still carry an open verification flag in

   1. **Whether an objection can be lodged**

An objection ("taxation objection") can be lodged against an ATO decision only where a
taxation law provides that a person who is dissatisfied with the decision may object in
the manner set out in Part IVC of the Taxation Administration Act 1953.[^1] For the
decision types in scope of this ruleset:

1. An **income tax assessment** (original or amended) can be objected to.[^2]

2. An objection against an **amended assessment** is limited to the alterations the
   amendment made — matters unchanged from the original assessment cannot be re-opened
   through the amended notice.[^3]

3. A **private ruling** can be objected to only while no assessment has been made for
   the period the ruling relates to; once an assessment exists, the objection must be
   made against the assessment instead.[^4]

4. A **GST assessment**, **FBT assessment**, **administrative penalty assessment** and
   **superannuation guarantee charge assessment** can each be objected to.[^5]

5. An **excess concessional contributions determination** can be objected to.[^5a]

6. A decision refusing to remit **shortfall interest charge (SIC)** can be objected to
   only where the SIC left unremitted is more than 20% of the tax shortfall.[^6]

7. A decision refusing to remit **general interest charge (GIC)** cannot be objected
   to.[^7]

8. The Commissioner's refusal of a request for **more time to object** (rule 1.3.2)
   cannot itself be objected to.[^8]

   2. **By when the objection must be lodged**

1. **Standard period — 60 days.** Unless a longer period below applies, the objection
   must be lodged within 60 days after the notice of the decision is given to the
   person. This is the period for administrative penalty assessments, superannuation
   guarantee charge assessments and objectable SIC remission decisions.[^9]

2. **Income tax assessments — 2 or 4 years.** The objection period is 2 years after the
   notice of assessment is given where items 1, 2, 3 or 3A of the table in subsection
   170(1) of the Income Tax Assessment Act 1936 apply to the assessment (broadly:
   individuals and small business entities on the standard amendment period), and
   4 years in any other case.[^10]

3. **Amended income tax assessments — the later of.** The objection period ends at the
   later of: (a) the end of the original assessment's 2-year or 4-year period (rule
   1.2.2), and (b) 60 days after the notice of the amended assessment is given.[^11]

4. **GST and FBT assessments — 4 years** after the notice of assessment is given.
   Confirmed 2026-07-06: GST net-amount assessments are an "assessable amount" under
   s 155-5(2)(a) TAA 1953 Sch 1, and s 14ZW(1)(bg) sets their objection period as the
   4-year "period of review" under s 155-35(2)(a) Sch 1.[^12]

5. **Private rulings — the later of** 60 days after the ruling is given, and the
   **same 2-year or 4-year period as rule 1.2.2** (not a flat 4 years — corrected
   2026-07-06) measured from the last day allowed for lodging the return for the
   income year the ruling relates to.[^13]

6. **Excess concessional contributions determinations — the same period as the related
   income tax assessment.** Corrected 2026-07-06: this is not a standalone 60-day
   period. The objection must be lodged within the same period the person would have
   to object to their income tax assessment for that income year under rule 1.2.2 (the
   2-year/4-year rule), measured from the notice date of that related assessment, not
   the determination's own notice date.[^13a]

7. **Counting the period.** A period expressed in days ends 60 calendar days after the
   start date; a period expressed in years ends on the same calendar day N years later
   (an anniversary of 29 February falls back to 28 February). An objection lodged **on**
   the final day is in time. *(VERIFY — end-day convention rests on the Acts
   Interpretation Act 1901, not on TAA/ITAA text.)*

   3. **Lodging out of time**

1. An objection lodged after the period in rule 1.2 has ended is treated as not lodged,
   unless it is lodged together with a written request asking the Commissioner to deal
   with it as if it were in time, stating fully and in detail the circumstances of the
   lateness.[^14]

2. This extension mechanism is treated as **not available** for late objections against
   private rulings. *(VERIFY — genuine drafting-interpretation question: the general
   extension mechanism in s 14ZW(2)–(4) relieves a duty to lodge "within" a period
   under s 14ZW(1), whereas the private-ruling deadline in s 14ZW(1A) is instead worded
   as an absolute bar — "cannot lodge … after the end of" — which may or may not fall
   within the extension mechanism's scope. The statute does not resolve this either way
   in as many words; needs a tax specialist's reading, not a re-read of the section.)*[^15]

3. If the Commissioner refuses the extension request, the person may apply to the
   Administrative Review Tribunal (ART) for review of the refusal.[^16]

   4. **Pathways where no objection right exists (or the right has lapsed)**

1. **GIC remission refusals:** complain to the Tax Ombudsman, seek judicial review in
   the Federal Court under the Administrative Decisions (Judicial Review) Act 1977, or
   re-request remission with further information.[^17]

2. **SIC remission refusals at or below the 20% threshold:** request an ATO internal
   review, complain, or seek judicial review.[^18]

3. **Private ruling where an assessment already exists (or the ruling period has
   lapsed):** object against the assessment within the assessment's own objection
   period.[^19]

4. **Refused extension request:** ART review (rule 1.3.3).

   5. **After the objection (context only — not encoded)**

A person dissatisfied with the Commissioner's objection decision may apply to the ART
for review, or appeal to the Federal Court, generally within 60 days of being served
with the objection decision.[^20] The ART replaced the Administrative Appeals Tribunal
on 14 October 2024.

2. **Terms**

| Term | Definition | References |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Taxation objection | An objection a person may make under a taxation law in the manner set out in Part IVC TAA 1953 | s 14ZL TAA 1953 |
| Notice given | The date the notice of the decision is given to (served on) the person — the start event for objection periods | s 14ZW TAA 1953 |
| Standard amendment period | The 2-year amendment window under items 1, 2, 3, 3A of the s 170(1) ITAA 1936 table (individuals; small business entities) — drives the 2-year objection period | s 170 ITAA 1936; s 14ZW(1)(aa) TAA 1953 |
| Private ruling | Binding ATO advice on how a tax law applies to a person's scheme or circumstances | Div 359 TAA 1953 Sch 1 |
| Excess concessional contributions determination | Commissioner's written determination of a person's excess concessional super contributions for a financial year | s 97-5 TAA 1953 Sch 1 |
| SIC / GIC | Shortfall interest charge (on amended-assessment shortfalls) / general interest charge (on unpaid amounts) | Div 280 TAA 1953 Sch 1; Part IIA TAA 1953 |
| Extension request | Written request lodged with a late objection asking the Commissioner to treat it as in time | s 14ZW(2)–(3), s 14ZX TAA 1953 |
| ART | Administrative Review Tribunal — external merits review body (replaced the AAT from 14 Oct 2024) | ART Act 2024 |

3. **PoC constraints and caveats**

* The ruleset covers only the eleven decision types in `research/decision-inventory.md`;
  other ATO decisions are out of scope and no answer should be inferred for them.
* The 2-year/4-year test is modelled through the `taxpayer_kind` input (individual /
  small business entity / other) as an approximation of the s 170(1) items 1–3A test;
  edge cases (e.g. individuals drawn into 4-year items by complex affairs,
  fraud-or-evasion unlimited amendment) are **not** modelled.
* "Notice given" is taken as the date on the notice as entered by the user; deemed
  service rules (postal delays, s 29 Acts Interpretation Act) are not modelled.
* Rule 1.2.6 (excess concessional contributions determinations) requires the notice
  date of a *different* decision (the taxpayer's income tax assessment for the same
  year) as an input, not just the determination's own date — the calculator must ask
  for this separately.
* Outputs are guidance for exploring Rules as Code, not legal or tax advice; any
  user-facing surface must carry the same reliance caveats the ATO's own decision
  tools use.
